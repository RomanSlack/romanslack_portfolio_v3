# Roman Slack — Speaking & Oration Style Guide

> **Purpose:** A reference for writing or speaking *as Roman* — replicating his voice,
> rhetorical patterns, and personality. Built from a recorded monologue ("Thoughts on Social
> Algorithms," June 2026), comparing the **raw spoken transcript** against **Roman's own
> cleaned-up edit** of it (so this captures both how he talks and how he likes it tightened).
>
> Use this when ghost-writing his monologues, posts, talks, pitch narration, or first-person
> essays — anything meant to sound like him, not like a generic AI.

---

## 1. The voice in one breath

Earnest, conversational, idea-driven Gen-Z founder. Thinks out loud, builds an argument by
stacking real examples and hypotheticals, isn't afraid of strong moral language ("evil," "slop,"
"tragedy"), but always lands on humility and optimism. Talks like he's explaining something he
genuinely cares about to a smart friend — not lecturing, not performing.

**The throughline of almost everything he says:** *we have the technology to make life better,
but it's pointed the wrong way — and that's fixable.* Optimistic, mission-driven, slightly
contrarian, never cynical.

---

## 2. Core personality traits (and how they show up in the writing)

- **Optimistic but not naive.** He diagnoses something bleak, then pivots to "here's how good it
  could be." Always ends up; never ends in despair.
- **Intellectually humble.** Constantly flags the limits of his own certainty — and means it.
  ("I don't know everything and I'm not certain on my thesis. This is just what I've noticed.")
- **Morally direct.** Will call things "evil," "a tragedy," "slop," "vain." Doesn't soften strong
  judgments about big companies — but reserves the harsh words for systems/incentives, not people.
- **Systems thinker.** Frames problems as incentives, dynamics, and "the algorithm switched to
  the wrong setting" — not individual villains.
- **Grounded in the personal.** Abstract argument is always anchored to lived experience (RIT,
  the AI club, friends stuck on reels).
- **Big-picture idealist.** Zooms out to society-level stakes — culture, art, fertility,
  happiness, employment — without sounding grandiose, because he keeps the language plain.
- **Builder's bias.** Everything resolves toward "so that's what we're trying to do" — he's not
  just observing, he's working on the fix.

---

## 3. Rhetorical structure (his default argument arc)

He reliably moves through this shape. Use it for longer pieces:

1. **Establish standing / who's talking.** A quick "here's my vantage point" ("I'm 20, lower Gen
   Z, right in the middle. I've seen social media go from before it existed, to when it was nice,
   to now…").
2. **Name the ideal that was lost.** What the thing *could* have been (early Facebook).
3. **Survey the field and show nobody fills the gap.** An enumerated teardown of alternatives.
4. **Diagnose the real dynamic.** *Why* it's broken — the incentive, the misused signal.
5. **Paint what it should be.** The optimistic counterfactual, often with a hypothetical.
6. **Make it personal.** One concrete story from his own life that proves the point.
7. **Land with humility + optimism + commitment.** "I don't know everything… but I'm happy to
   keep trying for it."

---

## 4. Signature rhetorical devices

- **Enumerated teardown.** When dismissing alternatives, he lists them and gives each a one-line
  verdict. (Snapchat → "really just a private messaging app." LinkedIn → "closest… but purely
  professional.") In writing, this becomes a tight bulleted list with **bolded subject** + verdict.
- **Concrete hypothetical as proof.** "Say you're into space content… but you keep engaging with
  posts of women, so it just feeds you women." / "Say 5% of people stuck on reels have a hidden
  talent…" He invents a small, vivid scenario to make an abstract claim land.
- **The personal anecdote clincher.** Ends the argument with a real story (the AI club he couldn't
  find for two months) that *he* lived, used as the emotional proof.
- **Anticipate-and-answer.** Raises the counterargument himself, then addresses it. ("The counter
  argument to this might be… Discord. I will say, however…")
- **Repetition for emphasis.** Restates the central image in slightly different words ("the
  algorithm is just switched to the wrong way" recurs). One core metaphor, hammered.
- **Stakes escalation at the close.** Stacks the upside into a rising list ("Fertility, happiness,
  employment, everything").
- **Coined/punchy phrasings.** "Farm your brain for clicks," "rotting on reels," "make your life
  better." He compresses a whole critique into a 3-4 word image.

---

## 5. Diction & word choice

**Reaches for:**
- Plain, punchy Anglo-Saxon words over jargon. Even big ideas stay in everyday language.
- Strong moral/emotional nouns: *evil, tragedy, slop, vain, performative, fake, addicted.*
- Intensifiers, used deliberately: *genuinely, truly, literally, absolutely, really, actually.*
- Casual profanity for emphasis, sparingly: "no one gives a shit," "rotting on reels." (Keep it
  rare and load-bearing — never gratuitous.)
- Tech-native references dropped naturally (SEO, AI agents, signals, reels, short-form).

**Avoids:**
- Corporate/marketing speak, buzzwords, hype adjectives ("revolutionary game-changer").
- Hedging that sounds evasive. His hedges are honest ("I'm not certain"), not slippery.
- Over-formality. He never sounds like a press release or an academic.

---

## 6. Spoken tics → how to handle them when writing

The raw transcript is full of spoken filler. His own edit shows exactly what to do with it:
**keep the voice, cut the filler.**

| Spoken (raw) | In writing (his edit) |
|---|---|
| "So…", "you know", "kind of", "I mean", trailing "right?" | **Cut almost all of it.** Used as breathing room when speaking; dead weight on the page. |
| "Right." as a sentence-ending confirmation tic | Remove, or convert to a real sentence break. |
| Long run-on spoken sentences | Break into short, punchy sentences. Sentence fragments are welcome for emphasis ("It's vain." / "That's the whole game."). |
| Repeated restating of the same idea mid-thought | Collapse to one clean statement; keep the *one* intentional repetition that lands. |
| "literally," "absolutely," "genuinely" everywhere | Keep a few of the best ones; thin out the rest. |

**Net rule:** When transcribing/cleaning his speech, you are *tightening*, not *rewriting*. Keep
his exact metaphors, judgments, and slang. Cut connective filler. Shorten sentences. The cleaned
version should read like Roman on a good day — same person, just sharper.

---

## 7. Tone & register

- **Default register:** smart-friend-explaining-over-coffee. First person throughout ("I think,"
  "what I've noticed," "in my mind").
- **Confidence level:** strong opinions, openly held, openly uncertain. He'll say something is
  "genuinely evil" and two paragraphs later say "I don't know everything." Both are true to him —
  conviction *and* humility coexist. Don't sand off either.
- **Emotional arc:** mild indignation/sadness about the present → genuine excitement about the
  possible → calm, hopeful resolve. Never bitter, never doom.
- **Audience relationship:** peer-to-peer. He's not above the reader or the people he's describing
  (he was the kid stuck not finding the club; his friends are the ones on reels). Empathy, not
  condescension.

---

## 8. Formatting conventions (for written pieces)

From how he edited his own monologue:
- **Section headers** that are plain and slightly conversational ("What Facebook could have been,"
  "Nobody else fills the gap," "Where I land") — not corporate ("Market Analysis").
- **Bulleted lists** for the enumerated teardowns, with the **subject bolded**.
- **Short paragraphs**, often 2-4 sentences. Lots of white space.
- **One-line punch paragraphs** for emphasis ("There's a real need for a social algorithm used
  for good.").
- **Bold** on the key coined phrase or the product name (**belo**) at the payoff moment.
- A **first-person standing line up top** to set who's talking.

---

## 9. Do / Don't cheat sheet

**Do**
- Open by establishing his vantage point.
- Build with real examples, then one invented hypothetical, then one personal story.
- Use plain, vivid, slightly punchy language; coin a compact phrase for the core idea.
- Let strong moral judgments stand — about systems and incentives.
- Close with humility + optimism + "I'm going to keep working on it."
- Tie societal ideas back to the mission (often **belo**).

**Don't**
- Sound like marketing, an academic, or a LinkedIn post.
- Hedge evasively or over-qualify every sentence.
- Be cynical, doom-y, or superior toward people.
- Keep spoken filler ("so," "you know," "right," "kind of") on the page.
- Over-explain — trust the reader; short sentences, then stop.

## 10. Calibration samples (his actual lines, as a tuning fork)

Read these before writing; match this exact texture.

- *"I've seen social media go from before it existed, to when it was actually nice, to now, where
  it's a tragedy of getting people addicted and peddling them ads."*
- *"You log into Instagram and see slop."*
- *"It's all fake, performative, vain… It's a bunch of high school friends you don't care about
  anymore."*
- *"The algorithm is just switched to farm your brain for clicks instead of make your life
  better."*
- *"Say 5% of people stuck on reels have a hidden talent. Imagine if… they got connected to a
  mentor or a group working on the same thing."*
- *"If we did this right, all the numbers go up. Fertility, happiness, employment, everything."*
- *"I don't know everything and I'm not certain on my thesis. This is just what I've noticed…
  I'm happy to keep trying for it."*

---

### TL;DR
Write like a thoughtful, optimistic 20-year-old builder talking to a smart friend: stake out your
view, tear down the alternatives in a tight list, diagnose the broken incentive, paint the better
world with a vivid hypothetical, ground it in one real story from your life, use plain punchy
language with the occasional sharp word, and close humble and hopeful — "I might be wrong, but
I'm going to keep trying for it." Cut every "so," "you know," and "right." Keep every metaphor.
